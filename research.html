Accreting Compact Objects

My main research area focuses on low-mass X-ray binaries - binary systems consisting of a low-mass star in orbit with either a neutron star or a black hole. The companion star transfers matter on to the primary via an accretion disc. LMXBs often exhibit transient outbursts, characterised by a huge increase in flux, followed by a gradual decay on varying timescales of days to months.

During my PhD I spent the majority of my time studying Swift J1753.5-0127, an unusual black hole system which outburst in 2005 and only returned to quiescence in late 2016. During it's near 12 year outburst it showed remarkable variability, as well as a short term excursion to a very low-luminosity soft accretion state. I am still very much involved with this fascinating system and I am very interested in learning the reason behind its unusually long period of activity. Something which I have worked on (with others) during my postdoc. For more information, see some of the papers I have published on Swift J1753.5-0127.

I have also been involved in the 2015 outburst of the famous black hole transient V404 Cygni, which was the first black hole to have its mass accurately measured. With the advance in technology we have had since the initial discovery in 1989, we have been able to study V404 Cyg in unprecedented detail. At Southampton, I was involved in a number of different aspects of this most recent outburst resulting in a Press Release that was picked up by many news outlets. I also, with extreme luck, managed to obtain a spectrum of V404 Cyg just thirteen hours before it went into outburst, making it the world's first 'pre-outburst' spectrum of a black hole. Recently, I have been involved with a Nature Astronomy paper which found that the base of the jet in V404 Cyg is elevated by 0.1 light seconds. You can read some of the papers that I have been involved with on V404 Cyg here.

Since my move to Edmonton, I have started to become more involved with binaries in globular clusters. The high stellar densities make them ideal locations for the dynamical formation of binaries and we find an abundance of X-ray binaries, millisecond pulsars and CVs in globular clusters. Telescopes like Chandra have discovered an population of quiescent low-mass X-ray binaries containing neutron stars, the X-ray spectra of which we can fit with model atmospheres, thereby constraining the neutron star radius. This has significant consequences for the neutron star equation of state, one of the important unanswered questions in modern day physics. I recently published a paper constraining the radius of the neutron star in the quiescent low-mass X-ray binary in M13.

Magnetic White Dwarfs

Recently, I successfully proposed a ~1 Ms Legacy Survey with NuSTAR to study magnetic cataclysmic variables. These systems consist of white dwarfs accreting material onto their poles from their companions along magnetic field lines. The infalling plasma forms a standing shock above the surface of the white dwarf, with a temperature directly related to its mass. Modelling of the X-ray spectrum enables us to derive the mass of the white dwarf. A large scale survey such as the one I am leading will allow us to infer a mass distribution of magnetic WDs and draw comparisons to single white dwarfs and non-magnetic CVs.

This mass distribution has important implications for binary evolution. It has been found that CVs seem to be more massive than post common envelope binaries ("pre-CVs"), as well as single white dwarfs. Why does this happen? Do CVs preferentially form above the period gap with larger masses? Or do they gain mass through accretion? Does this mass distribution hold for magnetic CVs? These are questions which we hope to be able to answer with the Legacy survey.
